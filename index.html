What is a mouse?

No, it's not the animal mouse, but the PC mouse. Most likely the thing you are holding if you are on the laptop or PC. The mouse is practically one of the most famous things in the world, and likely one of the things someone would list down when naming computer components.

In this wbepage, we are diving into the history, types, and components of the mouse.

History of Mice

Now, where did the mouse come from? Many people do not really bother checking the history of the mouse. Most people assume it is an invention, which, yes, it is.

Now, the first mouse was originally a military secret; back in 1946, the first mouse was created as a part of a British Navy Project about tracking aircrafts on the radar. The mouse's design was rough and basic: a ball placed on top of spinning metal discs with wires.

Since it was a military secret, it only became known 2 decades later.

The first actual mouse was created by Douglas Engelbart and his team at the Stanford Research Institute (SRI) in 1964. There were a few prototypes before 1964, but the 1964 was the first mouse officially. It looked like a wooden box with some wheels underneath the box that moved in perpendicular directions, which would move the cursor on screen.

The German company Telefunken released the first trackball based mouse named the Rollkugel or “rolling ball” in English. The mouse was a scaled down version of what the British Navy had been working on, its design had a single button and was used for drawing vector graphics.

In 1972, Bill English, a computer engineer working for Xerox Palo Alto Research Centre, created the “ball mouse”. It was part of the Xerox Alto computer, the first personal system with a graphical user interface. In this attempt, there were no wheels but a ball to move the mouse in any direction. The ball was in contact with axles attached to some rollers. With every movement, the axles produced electrical pulses sent to sensors that registered the direction the mouse was moving in. It was supposedly an iteration of the rolling ball mouse.

1983 was an important year for the mouse. Microsoft updated MS-DOS program Microsoft Word, creating the first PC-compatible mouse, then, Apple, Atari, and Commodore jump on the wave as well. And just one year later, Apple released the first Macintosh and that was a definitive step to popularize the device. The developments during the 90’s gave us ergonomic changes and the optical mouse. Finally, in 1999, it entered the market. A more resistant mouse that swiftly moved on surfaces like pads, tabletops, thanks to its optical sensors. In 2004, Logitech got the spotlight with its laser mouse. Its laser beam offered much more accurate tracking because it could identify surfaces under the mouse more precisely than LEDs.

The word 'mouse' was named as such as it looked similarly to a mouse with a tail, leading to the name 'mouse'. The wire 'tail' originally came out under the user's wrist when using the mouse.

Components of the Mouse

The shell, or casing of the mouse, which is the overall body of the mouse. (1)

The buttons, left and right buttons, which are used for clicking. You can feel it when holding the mouse and feeling a slight depth when holding the mouse. (2)

The receiver, which could be the USB stick that comes together from a wireless mouse, or a long wire from a wired mouse. (3)

The scroll wheel, the wheel between the buttons, which allows the user to scroll up or down a webpage. There is also a third button added to the scroll wheel. Touching it would turn the mouse cursor to a wheel that moves depending on which half of the page the cursor is in. (4)

Finally, the sensor, which track the actual mouse's movements. (5)
